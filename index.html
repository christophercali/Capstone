The Charge Forward: An Assessment of Electric Vehicle Charging Infrastructure in New York City

The US government is actively supporting the transition to Electric Vehicles (EVs) through various incentives and regulations with the recent passing of the Bipartisan Infrastructure Bill and Inflation Reduction Act. A recent press release by the Biden-Harris administration sets a goal of making EVs (electric vehicles) account for at least 50% of new car sales by 2030.

For the transition to fully electric vehicles to be successful, it is essential that the charging infrastructure supports the rollout by being widespread and easily accessible. New York City encounters various challenges to improving and increasing EV Charging infrastructure and is implementing different policies and goals to help spur growth.

Where does NYC stand in its efforts to establish accessible and widespread Electric Vehicle charging infrastructure? Is the City doing enough to increase infrastructure growth to sustain an increase in EVs on the road?

Currently, the city does not have enough charging stations to sustain an influx of electric vehicles.

Before exploring the Department of Energy data on EV Charging Stations below, it is important to understand that there are three types of EV chargers: Level 1, Level 2, and DC Fast Chargers (Level 3). Each level provides different speeds for a full charge:

  • Level 1: 24+ Hours
  • Level 2: 4-10+ Hours
  • DC Fast: 15 mins to 1+ Hour

Due to its inefficiency, Level 1 chargers are excluded from analysis below.



Common and Unique Challenges

"Filling up" using a gas pump is much quicker than "charging up" an electric vehicle.

A single gas pump will "fill up" 3X as many vehicles as a single DC Fast Charger, assuming 5 minutes at the pump versus 15 at the charger.
And that is being generous to the charger.



There are many more individual gas pumps than there are DC Fast Chargers.

A conservative estimate of gas pumps in NYC puts it at nearly 3,500. The Department of Energy Data gives a total of 170 DC Fast Chargers.

New York City needs to increase the amount of DC Fast Chargers that are publicly available, so that drivers can speedily get back on the road when they hit empty.


Furthermore, New York City is the most densely populated city in the United States.

NYC's densely packed population makes it more difficult to implement an efficient and widespread electric vehicle (EV) charging network. Actions by the City of New York to Improve Infrastructure

In New York City, the local government is taking steps to increase the uptake of EVs. The City's Department of Transportation (DOT), Taxi & Limousine Commission (TLC), and Department of Citywide Administrative Services, along with private companies, are working towards an all-electric future. Click around the buttons below to see what they are working on.

Taxi & Limousine Commission

TLC's Commissioner Do and Mayor Eric Adams announced the Green Rides Initiative, which will require that all High-Volume For-Hire Vehicles (Uber & Lyft) be zero-emission or wheelchair accessible by 2030. This will impact an estimated 100,000 heavily used for-hire vehicles. Uber and Lyft are both in support of this initiative.

TLC has phased the benchmarks for a "smooth and efficient transition to a cleaner, more accessible fleet." Beginning in 2024, five percent of all high-volume (Uber & Lyft) trips will need to be through vehicles that are either zero-emission or wheelchair accessible. The benchmark will then rise to 15% in 2025 and 25% in 2026. In 2027 it will increase to 40%, then rise yearly by 20 percentage points until the end of the decade, when 100 percent of all rides will be under the guidelines.

The initiative includes wheelchair accessible vehicles because manufacturers have not yet created zero-emission/electric wheelchair accessible cars. It is possible this will change over the next few years as the initiative rolls out and newer technology comes to market.

Department of Citywide Administrative Services (DCAS)

The Department of Citywide Administrative Services oversees the City vehicle fleet. They announced at a City Council Committee Meeting that their current goals are to electrify the light and medium duty fleets by 2035 and the heavy duty and specialized emergency equipment by 2040. DCAS operates more than 30,000 active vehicles, making it the largest municipal fleet in the country.

By the end of 2023, the City will operate more than 5,000 EVs.

The focus for DCAS electric charging is City EV fleet vehicles, but DCAS also currently offers 11 fast chargers at Parks and DCAS properties open to the general public. DCAS is planning at least 16 additional fast chargers for general access in the next year. Sites will include public parking areas at Van Cortlandt Park, Forest Park Bandshell, and World's Fair Marina.

Department of Transportation

The DOT has a goal of creating a network of 40,000 L2 chargers citywide and 6,000 DC Fast Chargers by 2030.

  • Curbside Level 2 Charging Pilot: In collaboration with ConEdison, DOT will install a network of 1,000 curbside charge points across the five boroughs by 2025, increasing to 10,000 by 2030. 100 of these started rolling out in August of 2021 as part of a pilot program.
  • Fast Charging at Municipal Parking Facilities: 13 Electric Vehicle Fast-Charging Hubs will be installed at Municipal Parking Facilities across the outer boroughs of NYC. Collectively, the hubs will feature roughly 50 fast-charging plugs.

PlaNYC: Getting Sustainability Done

In April of 2023, the city released a nearly 200-page report outlining climate goals, efforts, and policies. The plan sets the following goals with respect to electric vehicles:
  • Ensure every New Yorker is no more than 2.5 miles from an electric vehicle fast charging hub by 2035
  • Mandate private parking garages and lots to make electric vehicle charging available by 2030
  • Transition taxis and for-hire vehicles to electric vehicles (See Taxi & Limousine Commission)
  • Electrify school buses by 2035

Private Investment in NYC

For there to be a successful rollout of electric vehicles, it is essential that the private sector play a role in improving accessible infrastructure.


  • Revel: As of December 2023, Revel has 3 fast charging hubs that have a total of 54 fast chargers in NYC. The company says it will have hubs in all 5 boroughs by 2024, which would bring the total number of chargers to 136.
  • Hertz: Hertz intends to create 100 new jobs to serve its growing New York presence, donate five electric vehicles (EV) to automotive technical schools run by the New York City Department of Education (DOE), and add up to 1,700 electric vehicles to its New York City fleet for leisure and business customers. The company will also partner with four public high schools to provide EV education and training opportunities for students.

Assessing PlaNYC & The Current Pipeline

The 2.5 Miles

As noted above on the PlaNYC button, the City has set a goal to have all New Yorkers live within 2.5 miles of a DC Fast Charger by 2035, not a very ambitious goal and one that seems attainable. For perspective, 2.5 miles is about the length of Central Park from 59th Street to 110th Street, quite a long distance.


Accomplishing this goal will not mean that we have a successful rollout of EV charging infrastructure in the City. It can take about an hour to walk 2.5 miles and driving that distance will take at least 20 minutes or more within the City depending on traffic. A better goal would be 1 mile, which would allow drivers faster and easier access to charging.

Ubers & Lyfts Going Fully Electric

The requirement to have all High Volume For Hire Vehicles be fully electric by 2030 is the most initially impactful effort by the City of New York to electrify the vehicles on the road. This initiative will create a lot of demand for chargers, but it does not necessarily lead to an increase in charging infrastructure.

As of May 2023, there were over 77,000 Uber and Lyft vehicles registered and the numbers are increasing. These vehicles are the most heavily used in the City, completing around 20 million total trips on monthly basis, per TLC.


Currently, less than 5% of those vehicles are fully electric, which means that throughout the next few years, there will be tens of thousands of new for-hire EVs on the road.

According to EValuateNY, there are nearly 20,000 fully electric vehicles on the road within NYC counties.

Ubers and Lyfts alone will increase the current EV numbers on the road in NYC by more than 300%.

Following this logic, that means that infrastructure should also grow by more than 300% by 2030.


The Private Sector

City officials, including Mayor Adams, have empahasized that for the expansion of EV charging infrastructure to be successful, it is important for the private sector to build. You can see below for the current breakdown of chargers by company.

Although there are some public commitments to improve publicly accessible chargers across the City, like we have from Revel, it is unclear if there is a plan across the industry for full-scale investment into NYC's charging infrastructure.

The Final Determination

Certainly, there is a strong need for improved EV charging access across the City and with a spur in EV sales and registrations coming, it is quite clear that we are not prepared for it in our current state.

The current efforts that are outlined above will help the City increase growth of EVs on the road, will add to the current infrastructure, and will encourage the private sector to ramp up their production of chargers.

Based on our current knowledge and the current commitments from the private sector, it does not seem like there is enough in the current pipeline to set a good foundation of publicly accessible infrasrtructure for the rollout of EVs.

There is time for the City and private sector to work up to this, but it is running out.
