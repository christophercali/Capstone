The Charge Forward: An Assessment of Electric Vehicle Charging Infrastructure in New York City

The future of vehicular travel is rapidly moving towards electric-powered vehicles, thanks to a surge of interest and investment in sustainable transportation. The US government is actively supporting this transition through various incentives and regulations with the recent passing of the Bipartisan Infrastructure Bill and Inflation Reduction Act. A recent press release by the Biden-Harris administration sets a goal of making EVs (electric vehicles) account for at least 50% of new car sales by 2030.

For the transition to fully electric vehicles to be successful, it is essential that the charging infrastructure supports the rollout by being widespread and easily accessible. New York City encounters unique challenges to improving and increasing Electric Vehicle Charging infrastructure due to its densely packed population.

This Capstone project seeks to evaluate where NYC stands in its efforts to establish accessible and widespread Electric Vehicle charging infrastructure.

In New York City, the focus of this project, the local government is taking steps to increase the uptake of EVs. The City's Department of Transportation (DOT), Taxi & Limousine Commission (TLC), and Department of Citywide Administrative Services, along with private companies, are working towards an all-electric future. See what they are working on below.

Taxi & Limousine Commission

TLC's Commissioner Do and Mayor Eric Adams announced the Green Rides Initiative, which will require that all High-Volume For-Hire Vehicles (Uber & Lyft) be zero-emission or wheelchair accessible by 2030. This will impact an estimated 100,000 heavily used for-hire vehicles. Uber and Lyft are both in support of this initiative.

TLC has phased the benchmarks for a "smooth and efficient transition to a cleaner, more accessible fleet." Beginning in 2024, five percent of all high-volume (Uber & Lyft) trips will need to be through vehicles that are either zero-emission or wheelchair accessible. The benchmark will then rise to 15% in 2025 and 25% in 2026. In 2027 it will increase to 40%, then rise yearly by 20 percentage points until the end of the decade, when 100 percent of all rides will be under the guidelines.

The initiative includes wheelchair accessible vehicles because manufacturers have not yet created zero-emission/electric wheelchair accessible cars. It is possible this will change over the next few years as the initiative rolls out and newer technology comes to market.

The Department of Citywide Administrative Services oversees the City vehicle fleet. Private Investment

By the end of 2023, Revel plans to build 5 additional charging superhubs in the City on top of the current one they have in Bed-Stuy, which would bring 136 new fast-charging stalls to the City.

    It's important to note that there are three different types of EV charging stations:
  • Level 1 Chargers: Through a common residential 120V AC outlet, takes over 24 hours to provide a full charge for an electric vehicle.
  • Level 2 Chargers:Higher-rate AC charging through 240V (in residential applications) or 208V (in commercial applications) electrical service, common for home, workplace, and public charging. 4-10+ hours for full charge.
  • DC Fast Chargers:Direct current fast charging (DCFC) equipment offers rapid charging and can get an EV charged up in 20 minutes to 1+ hour.

    • For this analysis, we will focus on Level 2 and DC Fast Chargers due to the inefficiency of Level 1 chargers.
